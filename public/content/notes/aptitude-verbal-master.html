⚠ Type 2 Subjunctive Trap

Type 2 conditional uses "were" for ALL subjects (subjunctive mood): "If I were you…" — not "was". "If she were taller…" — not "was". The subjunctive "were" signals the condition is hypothetical. GATE sentence correction tests this frequently.

Articles — Complete Rule Set

a / an / the — When to Use What

a/an (indefinite): "an" before VOWEL SOUNDS, not vowel letters. "an MBA" (/em/), "a university" (/juː/), "an hour" (/aʊər/), "a one-time offer" (/wʌn/).
the (definite): Use when: (1) mentioned before, (2) unique in context, (3) superlatives, (4) specific geographic features (the Ganges, the Himalayas, the USA).
No article: languages (Hindi), sports (cricket), subjects (mathematics), abstract nouns generally (Love is powerful. Time is money.).

Chapter L
Reading Comprehension — Strategy & Question-Type Grid

L.1   The Complete RC System GATE Tested

4-Step RC Protocol

Step 1 (30 sec): Scan all questions FIRST. Know what you're hunting for — main idea? inference? tone? specific fact?
Step 2: Read passage actively. Mentally tag each paragraph's function: "This defines X / This challenges Y / This gives evidence for Z".
Step 3: For each question, return to the exact passage location. Ground every answer in specific text.
Step 4: Eliminate: (a) extreme language ("always", "never", "only"), (b) true-but-not-in-passage, (c) direct contradictions of the passage.

Question TypeWhat It AsksHow to AnswerMain Trap
Main Idea / ThemeWhat is the passage primarily about?Find the idea supported by EVERY paragraph, not just one sectionA specific detail that appears prominently but doesn't represent the whole
Specific FactWhat does the passage explicitly state?Locate and verify from the passage. Answer must be directly stated.A logical inference that follows from the fact but isn't the fact itself
InferenceWhat must follow logically?Not explicitly stated but required by the passage. One step of logic from what IS stated.Using general knowledge not grounded in the passage
Author Tone/AttitudeWhat is the author's stance toward X?Identify loaded words: "unfortunately", "remarkably", "puzzlingly" → these signal stanceYour own opinion, or what you'd expect the tone to be
Vocabulary in ContextWhat does word X mean in paragraph N?Read the full sentence. Substitute each option back — which preserves the meaning?The most common everyday meaning of the word (context often overrides it)
Title / Best HeadingWhich heading covers the whole passage?Same test as main idea — must cover all paragraphs, not just oneA specific detail elevated to a title; or too-broad generalisation
Strengthen / WeakenWhich statement supports / undermines the argument?Identify the author's core claim. Find the option that most directly attacks or supports it.Options that affect a peripheral point, not the central claim
⚠ The #1 RC Trap — "True but Not in Passage"

The option states something factually correct about the topic area — but it's drawn from general knowledge, not this passage. Rule: every answer must be traceable to specific text in the passage. The moment you think "this is generally true", you've found the trap.

Eliminate absolute language first: Options with "always", "never", "all", "none", "completely", "exclusively", "only" are almost always wrong. Authors hedge: "tends to", "in many cases", "often suggests". Unless the passage itself uses identical absolute language, eliminate these in your first pass. This typically eliminates 1–2 options immediately.

Inference ≠ Fact ≠ Assumption: Specific-fact answer = directly stated. Inference = one logical step beyond what's stated. Assumption = fills a gap the passage takes for granted. GATE uses all three in the same RC set. Ask: "Is this stated word-for-word? (Fact) → Is this required by what's stated? (Inference) → Does this fill a gap? (Assumption)".

M.1   Argument Anatomy & Question Types GATE Tested

Anatomy of an Argument

Working Example — all question types in one argument:

Premise: "All engineers who practise problems daily improve." Conclusion: "Aryan will improve." Assumption: Aryan is an engineer who practises problems daily. [UNSTATED — the gap] Strengthen: "Aryan solves 5 problems every single day." [makes assumption explicit and stronger] Weaken: "Aryan only practises on weekends, not daily." [attacks the assumption] Inference: "Engineers who practise daily do improve." [restated from premise — directly follows]
Question TypeWhat to DoDefinitive Test
Find the AssumptionIdentify the unstated bridge between premises and conclusionNegation test: negate each option — if the conclusion collapses, that's the assumption
StrengthenAdd a fact that makes the conclusion more likely to be trueMust support the premise→conclusion link, not just be related to the topic
WeakenAdd a fact that makes the conclusion less likely to be trueMust attack the core claim, not a peripheral detail
InferenceWhat must be true if the premises are true?The conclusion follows necessarily — not just probably or reasonably
Identify ConclusionFind what the author is actually claimingLook for "therefore", "hence", "so", "thus", "consequently" — they flag conclusions

Negation test for assumptions: Negate your candidate. "Aryan practises daily" → negated: "Aryan does NOT practise daily." Does the conclusion ("Aryan will improve") still hold? No — the entire argument falls apart. Confirmed assumption. If the conclusion still holds after negation → that option is NOT the assumption.

M.2   Logical Fallacies — Complete GATE/Placement List Interview Level

Ad Hominem
Attack the person making the argument, not the argument itself.
Example: "His design is flawed — he barely passed his degree." (Attacking the person, not the design.)
Straw Man
Misrepresent the opponent's position, then attack the misrepresented version.
Example: "He wants fewer tests — so he doesn't care about software quality at all." (Exaggerated misrepresentation.)
False Dichotomy
Present only two options when more actually exist.
Example: "Either we migrate to cloud or we go bankrupt." (Other options clearly exist.)
Post Hoc (Post hoc ergo propter hoc)
A preceded B, therefore A caused B. Confuses temporal sequence with causation.
Example: "Bugs increased after the new hire joined — he must be causing them." (Correlation ≠ Causation.)
Circular Reasoning
The conclusion is used as a premise — the argument goes around in a circle.
Example: "This code is correct because it has no bugs. It has no bugs because it is correct."
Hasty Generalisation
Broad conclusion drawn from too few or non-representative examples.
Example: "Two toppers used Vim — therefore all serious coders should use Vim."
Appeal to Authority
Claim is true merely because an expert said so, without independent evidence.
Example: "This architecture is optimal — the CTO said so." (No supporting reasoning given.)
Slippery Slope
Assume one event inevitably leads to extreme consequences without justification.
Example: "If we allow one exception, the entire codebase will become unmaintainable."
Bandwagon
Something is right because everyone does it.
Example: "Every company is switching to microservices, so we should too." (Popularity ≠ correctness.)

Chapter O
Direction Sense & Distance Problems

O.1   Coordinate Tracking Method GATE + Placements

Compass System & Turn Rules

A man faces North. He walks 6 km North, turns right and walks 4 km, then turns right and walks 6 km. How far and in what direction from his starting point?

Start at (0,0), facing North. Walk 6 km North → (0, 6) Turn right (→ face East). Walk 4 km East → (4, 6) Turn right (→ face South). Walk 6 km South → (4, 0) Net x = 4 (East), Net y = 0 Displacement = √(4² + 0²) = 4 km Direction = Due East ← stays at y=0, positive x Answer: 4 km East of starting point.
Shadow Direction Rules

The sun is always the reference, never the person's facing direction.
Morning: sun rises in East → shadows fall West (away from sun).
Evening: sun sets in West → shadows fall East.
Noon (India): sun roughly overhead/slightly South → shortest shadows, falling slightly North.

P.1   Tree Notation & All Relation Types GATE + Placements

The Only Method That Works — Always Draw a Tree

Never solve blood relation problems in your head. Draw a family tree for every problem, even simple ones. The cognitive load of tracking 3+ relationships mentally almost always leads to errors.

Standard Two-Generation Family Tree with Notation

A is the mother of BA is female. A is B's parent. Draw A above B with vertical line.
A is the son of the only daughter of BB's only daughter = A's mother. B is A's maternal grandfather.
A is the mother's brother of BA is male. A's sister = B's mother. A is B's maternal uncle.
A's father's only sonIf father has only one son, that son IS A. "A's father's only son = A himself." Self-reference!

⚠ Gender Traps

Mark gender ONLY when explicitly stated. Many GATE questions leave the subject's gender ambiguous ("A is B's sibling") and rely on you assuming. If gender is unknown, the answer may be "brother or sister" — watch for this option.

"Only son" / "only daughter" are self-reference traps. "A's father's only son" = A himself. "A's mother's only daughter" = A herself (if female). Identify these immediately — they collapse the chain.

Chapter Q
Syllogisms & Venn Reasoning

Q.1   Valid Patterns, Invalid Patterns & Venn Method GATE Tested

The Cardinal Rule

A conclusion is valid ONLY IF it holds in every possible valid Venn diagram that satisfies the premises. If you can draw even one valid diagram where the conclusion fails → the conclusion DOES NOT FOLLOW. Draw the minimum-overlap diagram to test whether "Some" conclusions can fail.

"All A are B" — Two Valid Configurations, Both Must Satisfy the Conclusion
Given PremisesValid ConclusionsInvalid — Never Conclude
All A are BSome B are A ✓   Some A are B ✓All B are A ✗   No A is B ✗
No A is BNo B is A ✓   Some A are not B ✓   Some B are not A ✓Some A are B ✗
Some A are BSome B are A ✓All A are B ✗   No A is B ✗
All A→B + All B→CAll A are C ✓   Some C are A ✓All C are A ✗
All A→B + No B is CNo A is C ✓   Some C are not A ✓Some A are C ✗
Some A→B + All B→CSome A are C ✓   Some C are A ✓All A are C ✗
Some A→B + Some B→CNo definitive conclusion about A and C. ANY claim about A and C is ✗
⚠ The Most Tested Invalid Conclusion

"Some A + Some B → nothing about A and C" — Draw it: A partially overlaps B, C partially overlaps B, but A and C may have zero intersection (their parts of B may be completely different subsets). "Some dogs are black, some black things are cars → some dogs are cars" is obviously wrong. Same logic applies everywhere.

Possibility vs Definite — Key Distinction

Possible: True in at least one valid Venn diagram. Definite: True in ALL valid diagrams. "Some B are A" is a definite conclusion from "All A are B". "All B are A" is a possible (but not definite) conclusion — it's valid in Config 2 but not Config 1. GATE often asks "which conclusion is a possibility?" vs "which definitely follows?"

Chapter R
Statement-Conclusion & Cause-Effect

R.1   Statement-Conclusion Reasoning GATE + Placements

The Core Test

A conclusion follows from a statement if it is a direct, necessary logical consequence — not an assumption, not general knowledge, and not an extreme extrapolation. Ask: "If the statement is true, must this conclusion be true, without adding any new information?"

StatementConclusion AConclusion BVerdict
Students who revised regularly scored above 80%.All high scorers revised regularly. ✗Regular revision improves scores for most students. ✓B follows. A reverses the relationship — not stated or required.
Company X increased R&D budget by 40%.Company X will launch new products. ✗Company X has invested more in research than before. ✓B follows (directly stated). A requires an assumption (R&D → new products — not guaranteed).
Literacy rate rose from 60% to 75% in a decade.Primary school enrollment increased. ✗The state made educational progress. ✓B follows. A requires a specific causal mechanism not stated in the data.

The "zero additional premise" test: A conclusion must not require any additional premise. If you find yourself thinking "this follows because [something extra]", that "something extra" is an assumption — and the conclusion does not follow on its own. Valid conclusions require zero extra information beyond what the statement provides.

R.2   Cause and Effect — Four Patterns Interview Level

Pattern 1: A → B (Direct)

A directly causes B. Heavy rainfall → flooding. The mechanism is direct and plausible. A precedes B in time.

Pattern 2: Common Cause

C → A and C → B. Both A and B happen because of a third factor. A and B are correlated but not causal of each other.

Pattern 3: Reversed

B → A (reverse of what appears obvious). Low humidity → static electricity. The "cause" may not be the more prominent event.

Pattern 4: Coincidence

A and B happened around the same time but are completely independent. Market rose and a celebrity married — no mechanism connects them.

3-Part Test for Genuine Causation

(1) Does A precede B in time?
(2) Is there a plausible, known mechanism connecting A to B?
(3) Have obvious confounding factors been ruled out?
All three must be satisfied before claiming A causes B. GATE cause-effect questions almost always hinge on one of these three conditions being missing.

Statement-Assumption — Negation Method

Assumption Rules

An assumption is unstated, taken for granted, and necessary for the statement or action to be rational.
Negation test: Negate the candidate. If the statement/action becomes pointless or self-contradictory → confirmed assumption. If the statement still makes sense after negation → it is not a necessary assumption.

Statement: "Advertise your product on social media to increase sales."

Assumption A: Social media advertising reaches potential buyers. Negate → "Social media does NOT reach potential buyers." If true, the advice makes no sense. ✓ IS an assumption. Assumption B: The product is not yet popular. Negate → "The product is already popular." The advice (advertise more) still makes sense. ✗ NOT an assumption.

Correct

An assumption is an UNSTATED bridge. If it's explicitly mentioned in the premises, it's a fact, not an assumption.

13
Wrong

Causation: Event A happened before Event B, so A must have caused B.

Correct

Post Hoc Fallacy. Temporal sequence is necessary but not sufficient for causation. Look for a mechanism.

14
Wrong

Coding: Assuming a fixed shift without checking the 2nd and 3rd letters.

Correct

Many codes use increasing shifts (+1, +2, +3) or mirror positions (27-p). Always verify the pattern.

15
Wrong

Directions: Right turn always means moving East.

Correct

Turns are relative to current facing. Face South → turn right → move West. Use coordinates (+x/-x).

16
Wrong

Blood Relations: Assuming gender based on a name (e.g., "Kiran").

Correct

Mark gender ONLY when explicitly stated by "son," "daughter," "wife," or "he/she."

17
Wrong

Blood Relations: "A's father's only son" means a third person.

Correct

Self-reference trap. If A is male, A's father's only son IS A himself.

18
Wrong

Statement-Conclusion: Adding common sense or external logic to the statement.

Correct

Conclusions must be 100% derived from the given statement with zero outside premises.

19
Wrong

Venn: "No A is B" and "No B is C" → "No A is C."

Correct

INVALID. A and C can overlap entirely and still not touch B.

20
Wrong

Argument: A "Possible Conclusion" must follow definitely.

Correct

"Possible" means it is true in at least ONE diagram. "Definite" means it is true in ALL diagrams. Check the question wording!
